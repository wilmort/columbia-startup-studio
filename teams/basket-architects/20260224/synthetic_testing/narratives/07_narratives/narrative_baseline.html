Researcher's Stance

The baseline is deliberately bad ‚Äî a generic, flat landing page with no brand voice, fake testimonials, and functional-only copy. Its purpose is to calibrate the floor so that V1 gains can be validated as real rather than AI grading generosity. The 7% conversion confirms the floor is low enough to be useful.

Executive Summary

Tested a deliberately mediocre landing page ('Track Your Experiences ‚Äî Remember Everything You Do') against 60 personas across 5 buckets. Conversion was 7%, resonance 17%, clarity nailed_it 0%. This establishes the scoring floor for all subsequent rounds.

Findings

  • Floor established at 7% conversion: Low enough to validate V1 lifts as genuine. Any version scoring above 15% is demonstrably better than generic copy.
  • Clarity at 0% nailed_it: No persona correctly identified what the product does. Generic framing ('Track Your Experiences') could describe 50 apps.
  • Fake testimonials detected: Multiple personas flagged social proof as 'obvious marketing copy' and 'generic.' Synthetic users catch inauthenticity.
  • Price anchoring failed: 50% rated pricing 'too expensive' ‚Äî without value framing, $12/month feels arbitrary.

Recommendations

  1. Replace generic hero with identity-driven framing: 'Your social life, ranked' or equivalent ‚Äî something only this product could say.
  2. Write specific copy: Use concrete examples (ratings, categories, scenarios) instead of abstract claims.
  3. Add real brand voice: The baseline reads like a template. The product has a distinctive voice ‚Äî use it.
  4. Fix testimonials: Replace generic quotes with specific, situation-driven social proof.

Qualitative Themes

  • Generic / could be any app (42 mentions): 'This sounds like every other tracking app.' 'Nothing distinguishes it.'
  • Fake testimonials (18 mentions): 'These don't sound like real people.' 'Obviously written by marketing.'
  • Unclear what it actually does (35 mentions): 'Is this a journal? A review site? A recommendation engine?'
  • Price without value (22 mentions): 'Why would I pay for this when Google Maps is free?'

Clarity Notes

0% nailed_it, 13% partial, 87% wrong. The generic framing completely fails to communicate the product's actual value proposition.
